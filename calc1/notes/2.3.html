2.3 - Calculating limits using limit laws

Two special limits:
  1. $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} x = a$
  2. For a constant $c$, $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} c = c$
(limit laws)

Suppose $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \;\; \text{and} \;\; \lim_{x \to a} g(x) $$ both exist, then the following are true:
  1. $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \left[ f(x) + g(x) \right] = \lim_{x \to a}f(x) + \lim_{x \to a}g(x)$
  2. $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \left[ f(x) \cdot g(x) \right] = \lim_{x \to a}f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a}g(x)$
Corollaries to Theorem 2.3.1:
  1. If $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a}g(x) \neq 0$, $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \left[ \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \right] = \frac{\lim_{x \to a}f(x)}{\lim_{x \to a}g(x)}$
  2. Let $c$ be any constant and $g(x)=c$, then using Thm 2.3.1 Rule 2, $$\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \left[ cf(x) \right] = c\lim_{x \to a}f(x)$$
  3. $$\begin{align*}\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \left[ f(x) - g(x) \right] &= \lim_{x \to a} \left[ f(x) + (-1)g(x) \right] \\ &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} \left[ (-1)g(x) \right] \tag{Thm 2.3.1 Rule 1} \\ &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \tag{Corollary 1} \end{align*}$$
  4. Letting $g(x)=f(x)$, and repeatedly apply Thm 2.3.1 Rule 2, $$\lim_{x \to a} \left[ f(x)^n \right] = \left[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \right]^n$$
  5. Using the previous corollary, $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} \left[ (\sqrt[n]{f(x)})^n \right] = \left[ \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} \right]^n$. Taking $n$th roots, we have: $$\lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} = \sqrt[n]{ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) }$$
  6. Letting $f(x)=x$, we have two special cases of the previous two corollaries, $$\lim_{x \to a} \left[ x^n \right] = a^n \;\; \text{and} \;\; \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{x} = \sqrt[n]{a}$$

Note: Theorem 2.3.1 and its corollaries hold for one-sided limits also.

Find $$\lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x^3 + 2x^2 -1}{5-3x}$$ (ans = -1/11)
(direct substitution property) If $f$ is a polynomial or a rational function and $a$ is in the domain of $f$, then $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) $$
Foreshadowing: continuous functions are those which satisfy the direct substitution property

If $f(x)=g(x)$ when $x\neq a$, then $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} g(x)$.

Find $\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)$ if $$f(x) = \begin{cases} \sqrt{x-4} & x \gt 4 \\ 8-2x & x \lt 4\end{cases}$$ (ans = 0)
If $f(x) \leq g(x)$ near $a$ (except possibly at $x=a$), then $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) \leq \lim_{x \to a} g(x)$$
(the Squeeze theorem) If $g(x) \leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ near $a$ (except possibly at $x=a$) and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L$, then $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$
Find $\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin(1/x)$. solution: use $-1 \leq \sin(1/x) \leq 1$ and multiply both sides by $x^2$. since $x^2 \to 0$, the function goes to 0.
