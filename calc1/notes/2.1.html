2.1 - The tangent and velocity problems

The secant line

Given a curve, $f$, and two points on the graph of this curve, $(x_1,f(x_1))$ and $(x_2,f(x_2))$, the secant is the straight line through these points.
Recall that the slope of the secant is found using the slope formula, $m = \frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1}$. The equation for the secant is found using the point-slope equation for a line: $y-f(x_1) = m (x-x_1)$
Let $f(x)=x^2$. Find the slope of the secant between when $x_1=1$, and when $x_2=0.9, 0.99, 1.1, 1.01$. (ans=1.9, 1.99, 2.01, 2.1) What is the slope of the tangent line when $x=1+h$? What happens when $h \to 0$?
The tangent line of a curve at a point, $(x,f(x))$ is easy to grasp intuitively, but how do we pin it down mathematically? Does the previous example suggest a way to give slope of this line by some limiting process?
The slope of the tangent line at a point is defined to be the limit of the slopes of the secant line approximations around the tangenet line point.

Velocity

Suppose the position of a moving particle at a time, $t$, is given by the curve, $f(t$). Given two points in time, $t_1$ and $t_2$, the average velocity, over this is interval in time is given by: $$ \text{average velocity} = \frac{\text{change in position}}{\text{change in time}} = \frac{f(t_2) - f(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} $$
Suppose a ball is thrown into the air, and its height over time is given by $f(t) = 1+ 3t - t^2$. What is its average velocity from $t_1=0$ to $t_2=1$? $t_2=0.1$? $t_2=h$? What happens when $h \to 0$?
The instantaneous velocity at a time, $t$, is the limit of average velocity about the time, $t$, as the length of time defining the average goes to zero.
In the last example what is instantaneous velocity when the ball is first thrown (i.e., when $t=0$)?
