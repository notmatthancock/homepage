2.2 - The limit of a function

Fuzzy definition: If $f(x)$ is close to $L$ whenever $x$ is sufficiently close to $a$, we say the limit as $x$ goes to $a$ of $f(x)$ is $L$. This is written as: $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L $$ or $$ f(x) \to L \; \text{as} \; x \to a $$ This definition is fuzzy because, we've not been precise about what we mean by "close" and "sufficiently close". Nonetheless, this definition will suffice for now.

Example

Let $f(x) = \frac{x-1}{x^2-1}$. Determine $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 1} f(x)$ by calculating $f(x)$ at values close to $x=1$.
ans: (0.9,0.5263), (0.99,0.5025), (0.999,0.5003), (1.001,0.4998), (1.01,0.4975), (1.1,0.4762)

Example

Let $f(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{x}$. Calculate the limit as $x \to 0$ by guessing values.
ans: (-0.1,0.99833), (-0.01,0.99998), (0.01,0.99998), (0.1,0.99833)


Moral: $f(x)$ does not have to exist at $x=a$ for the limit to exist at $x=a$.

Example

Let $f(x) = \sin(\pi/x)$. Determine the limit by guessing.

$f(1/n)=0, \forall n$, and $1/n \to 0$. On the other hand, $f(2/n) = \pm 1, \forall n$, and $2/n \to 0$. Thus, $f(x)$ has no proper limit as $x \to 0$.


Moral: guessing values could lead to improper conclusions.

Example

Let $\displaystyle H(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x \geq 0 \\ 0 & x \lt 0 \end{cases}$. $H(x)$ is sometimes called the unit step function or Heaviside function. Clearly by inspecting the graph, $H(x)$ has no limit at $x=0$.


One-sided limits

In the previous example, $H(x)$ approaches $1$ if we approach $x=0$ from the right, and $H(x)$ approaches $0$ if we approach $x=0$ from the left. We denote these one-sided limits by: $$ \lim_{x \to 0^+} H(x) = 1 \;\; \text{and} \;\; \lim_{x \to 0^-}H(x) = 0 $$ or in general: $$ \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = A \;\; \text{and} \;\; \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = B $$ This means $f(x)$ goes to $A$ if $x$ approaches $a$ from the right, while $f(x)$ goes to $B$ when $x$ goes to $a$ from the left. Hence, the limit exists at $a$ if and only if: $$ \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) $$

Infinite limits

If the value of a function is unbounded if we approach some value $x=a$ from the left or right, we say the limit is $\infty$. Strictly speaking, this is just convention, and the limit still does not exist at $x=a$.

Example

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$. Then, we would say say: $$ \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) = -\infty \;\; \text{and} \;\; \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) = +\infty $$

Example

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2}$. Then $\lim_{x \to 0} = +\infty$, but again, strictly speaking, this is just notation, and if asked whether or not the limit exists, the answer is no.



Moral: left and right limits might agree, but the limit should still be a finite number to say the limit exists.
