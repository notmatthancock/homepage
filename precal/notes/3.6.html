3.6 — Complex Zeros — Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Suppose $f(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $n$ with complex coefficients. Then (counting multiplicity) $f$ has exactly $n$ roots.

This also means a polynomial of degree $n$ may always be decomposed into factored form, $$ f(x) = A(x-r_1)(x-r_2)\ldots(x-r_n) $$ assuming we know the $n$ roots, $r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_n$.
Factor the function, $f(x) = x^2 + 1$, completely.
$f(x) = (x+i)(x-i)$
Factor the function, $f(x) = x^3 + 1$, completely.
$\displaystyle f(x) = \left(x+1\right)\left(x-\left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\right)\left(x-\left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\right)$

Conjugate Zeros

If $f(x)$ is a polynomial with real coefficients and $z$ is a complex number such that $f(z) = 0$ ($z$ is a root), then $\bar{z}$ is also a root of $f$.

Determine all remaining zeros of a polynomial with real coefficients and degree 5 having zeros $3, 4-3i, -2+5i$.
$4+3i, -2-5i$
Select from the following the polynomial with real coefficients of degree 5 and zeros of $1, 2-4i, -3+i$.
  1. $(x-1)(x+2+4i)(x+2-4i)(x+3+i)(x+3-i)$
  2. $(x-1)(x-2+4i)(x-2-4i)(x+3+i)(x+3-i)$
  3. $(x-1)(x+2+4i)(x+2-4i)(x-3+i)(x-3-i)$
  4. $(x-1)(x-2+4i)(x-2-4i)(x-3+i)(x-3-i)$
