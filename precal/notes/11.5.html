11.5 — The Binomial Theorem

The Binomial Coefficient

$$ \begin{pmatrix} n \\ j \end{pmatrix} = \frac{n!}{j!(n-j)!} $$ This is read as "$n$ choose $j$" because it is the number of ways to choose $j$ things from $n$ when order doesn't matter. For instance, maybe there are 5 types of fruit on display at the super market, but I only have room for 2 fruit to put in my basket. Then there are: $$ \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{5!}{2!(5-2)!} = 10 $$ possible pairs of fruit I could pick.

Binomial Expansions and the Binomial Theorem

Consider expanding:

$ (a+b)^2 $

or

$ (a+b)^3 $

... or

$ (a+b)^n $

The binomial theorem says that: $$ (a+b)^n = \sum_{j=0}^n \begin{pmatrix} n \\ j \end{pmatrix} a^j b^{n-j} = \sum_{j=0}^n \begin{pmatrix} n \\ j \end{pmatrix} a^{n-j} b^j $$

Digression About the Number, $e$, Continuous Interest, and the Binomial Theorem

Recall that the compound interest model is $$ A = P(1+\frac{r}{n})^{nt} $$ We said that the continuous interest model arises from the compound interest model when we let the number of times the loan is compounded become instantaneous, i.e., $$ P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \longrightarrow Pe^{rt}, \;\; \text{when} \;\; n \longrightarrow \infty $$ Taking $P=t=1$, we have more simply that $$ \left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{n} \longrightarrow e^{r}, \;\; \text{when} \;\; n \longrightarrow \infty $$ By applying the binomial theorem to the left side, one finds that: $$ e^r = \sum_{j=0}^n \frac{r^j}{j!}, \;\; \text{when} \;\; n \longrightarrow \infty $$ because the factorial terms involving $n$ form a rational function in $n$ with horizontal asymptote of $1$. This means these terms go away when $n$ gets really big.

This is a very big result. In terms you will learn in calculus, this says that: $$ e^x = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1+\frac{x}{n}\right)^n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{j=0}^n \frac{x^j}{j!} $$
