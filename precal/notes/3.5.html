3.5 — Complex Numbers

The Number $i$

$i$ is the imaginary unit defined to be the number such that $i^2 = -1$.

Complex Numbers

A complex number is a number with two parts: a real part and an imaginary part. Any complex number can be written as: $$ z = a + bi \;\; \text{(standard form)} $$ $a$ is the real part, and $b$ is the imaginary part.

Note that any real number can be viewed as a complex number with zero imaginary part (e.g., $2 = 2 + 0\cdot i$). So, the real numbers are contained within the complex numbers (like how integers are contained within fractions).

Write the following in standard form ($a+bi$):
  1. $4i-(3+2i)+(1-3i)$
  2. $(5+6i)(2-i)$
  1. $-2-i$
  2. $16+7i$

The Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of a complex number, $z=a+bi$, is denoted: $$ \bar{z} = a-bi $$ A complex number multiplied by its complex conjugate is always purely real since: \begin{align*} z\bar{z} &= (a+bi)(a-bi) \\ &= a^2 - abi + abi - b^2 i^2 \\ &= a^2 + b^2 \\ \end{align*}
Write the following in standard form ($a+bi$)
  1. $(3+4i)(3-4i)$
  2. $\frac{1-i}{1+4i}$
  1. $25$
  2. $\frac{-3-5i}{17}$
